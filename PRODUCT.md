# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Three overlapping audiences, none subordinate to the others:

- **Informed voter / resident** — wants to understand who holds power and money in Harris County. Arrives with a specific question (who endorsed X? where does the money go?) and needs an answer fast.
- **Political operative / organizer** — uses the site as a research tool for campaigns, endorsements, and opposition research. Values density and accuracy over hand-holding.
- **Journalist / researcher** — pulls data to write about local politics. Needs shareable cards, clear sourcing, and exportable insight.

All three are smart, skeptical, and will bounce immediately if the site feels generic, slow, or untrustworthy.

## Product Purpose

The Harris County Project is a civic media brand built around data tools. It makes the machinery of local power — campaign finance, legislation, budgets, endorsements, maps — legible to anyone willing to look. It exists because this information is technically public but practically inaccessible. The site closes that gap.

Success looks like: a resident opens it, finds the thing they were looking for in under 30 seconds, and shares it.

## Brand Personality

**Bold · Direct · Local**

Voice: confident without being partisan, sharp without being snide. It speaks like someone who has done the homework and isn't apologizing for having opinions about what the numbers mean. Houston-specific in every detail — not a generic civic tech template dropped into a new zip code.

Emotional goal: the user should feel equipped. Not lectured, not dazzled — equipped.

## Anti-references

- **Generic civic tech clones** (OpenSecrets-style, mySociety-style) — utility-first, no personality, looks like a government subcontractor built it.
- **AI slop** — cookie-cutter layouts, Inter everywhere, purple gradients, cards inside cards inside cards, bounce easing. Nothing that could have been generated without knowing this city.

## Design Principles

1. **Clarity is the product** — if a data point takes more than one look to parse, the design failed. Hierarchy, contrast, and spacing exist to serve speed of understanding, not aesthetics.
2. **Local or nothing** — every design choice should feel rooted in Houston. The typefaces, the colors, the copy register. Generic is the enemy.
3. **Earned authority** — the data earns trust; the design doesn't need to perform credibility. No institutional theater, no fake gravitas. Let the numbers do the work.
4. **Density by default, breathing room by exception** — information-dense layouts are respectful of the user's time. White space is earned, not default.
5. **Editorial conviction** — the site has a point of view. Design choices commit; they don't hedge.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

- WCAG AA minimum. Body text contrast ≥ 4.5:1 against all backgrounds.
- All interactive elements keyboard-accessible.
- Reduced motion: animations optional (respects `prefers-reduced-motion`).
- Maps and charts have text-based fallbacks or summaries.
